# Instrument Profiles for Live Sound Mixing

Detailed per-instrument profiles with frequency ranges, EQ moves, compression settings, gate parameters, pan position, and fader level relative to lead vocal. All values are professional starting points for live concert mixing on the Behringer Wing Rack.

## Kick Drum

### Frequency Ranges
- **Fundamental**: 40-80 Hz. The deep "thud" and weight of the kick.
- **Body**: 80-200 Hz. Fullness and power.
- **Boxiness**: 300-500 Hz. Almost always needs a cut. This is where cardboard-like resonance lives.
- **Beater attack**: 2-5 kHz. The "click" of the beater hitting the head.
- **Air/presence**: 6-10 kHz. Modern pop/rock kick brightness.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 30 Hz, 18 dB/oct
- Boost: 60 Hz, +3 dB, Q=1.5 (sub weight)
- Cut: 400 Hz, -4 dB, Q=2.0 (remove boxiness)
- Boost: 2.5 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (beater click)
- Shelf: 8 kHz, +1.5 dB (air, optional)

### Compression
- Ratio: 4:1
- Threshold: -12 dB
- Attack: 20 ms (lets beater transient through for punch)
- Release: 80 ms (recovers before next hit at 150 BPM)
- Detector: Peak
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Threshold: -30 dB
- Attack: 0.5 ms (capture full transient)
- Hold: 50 ms
- Release: 200 ms (match kick drum decay)
- Range: 40 dB (full gate closure)

### Pan Position
- **Center (0)**. Kick drum is always dead center. It anchors the low-frequency foundation of the mix and must be mono-compatible for all PA configurations including mono subs.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-3 to -6 dB** below lead vocal. The kick should be felt and heard as the rhythmic foundation but never overpower the vocal. In rock/metal, push closer to -3 dB; in pop/acoustic, pull back to -6 dB.

## Snare Drum

### Frequency Ranges
- **Fundamental**: 150-250 Hz. The body and weight of the snare.
- **Boxiness**: 600-900 Hz. Cardboard resonance to cut.
- **Crack**: 3-5 kHz. The sharp stick-on-head transient.
- **Snare wires**: 8-12 kHz. The sizzle and shimmer of the snare wires.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 80 Hz, 18 dB/oct
- Boost: 200 Hz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (body)
- Cut: 800 Hz, -3 dB, Q=2.0 (boxiness)
- Boost: 3.5 kHz, +2.5 dB, Q=1.5 (crack)
- Shelf: 10 kHz, +2 dB (wire sizzle)

### Compression
- Ratio: 4.5:1
- Threshold: -10 dB
- Attack: 5 ms
- Release: 100 ms
- Detector: Peak
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Threshold: -28 dB
- Attack: 0.5 ms
- Hold: 40 ms
- Release: 150 ms
- Range: 30 dB

### Pan Position
- **Center (0)**. Like the kick, the snare is a core rhythmic element and must remain dead center for maximum impact and mono compatibility.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-6 to -8 dB** below lead vocal. The snare provides rhythmic punctuation. It should cut through cleanly without dominating. In rock/metal, closer to -6 dB; in jazz or acoustic settings, -8 dB or lower.

---

## Toms (Floor Tom, Rack Toms)

### Frequency Ranges
- **Fundamental**: 60-120 Hz (floor tom) / 100-200 Hz (rack toms)
- **Body**: 120-300 Hz
- **Boxiness**: 300-600 Hz
- **Attack**: 2-4 kHz. Stick impact definition.
- **Air**: 5-8 kHz

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 60 Hz (floor tom) / 80 Hz (rack toms)
- Boost: 100 Hz, +2.5 dB, Q=1.5 (fundamental body)
- Cut: 400 Hz, -3.5 dB, Q=2.0 (boxiness)
- Boost: 3 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (stick attack)

### Compression
- Ratio: 4:1
- Threshold: -12 dB
- Attack: 10 ms
- Release: 100 ms
- Detector: Peak
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Threshold: -32 dB
- Attack: 1 ms
- Hold: 60 ms
- Release: 250 ms
- Range: 40 dB

### Pan Position
- **Spread across the stereo field (audience perspective)**:
  - High tom: 10-20 left
  - Mid tom: 10-20 right
  - Floor tom: 30-40 right
- Toms are panned to create a natural stereo image of the drum kit as seen from the audience. The spread follows high-to-low, left-to-right from audience perspective.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-10 to -15 dB** below lead vocal. Toms are typically muted or gated when not in use and only heard during fills. When they hit, they should be impactful but brief. Unmute/open gate only during tom passages in rehearsed shows if possible.

---

## Hi-Hat

### Frequency Ranges
- **Fundamental ring**: 300-600 Hz (usually bleed from other drums)
- **Body**: 600-2 kHz
- **Definition**: 3-6 kHz. The character of the hi-hat sound.
- **Shimmer**: 8-16 kHz. Brightness and air.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 200 Hz, 18 dB/oct (aggressive — remove all drum bleed)
- Cut: 400 Hz, -4 dB, Q=1.0 (bleed cleanup)
- Boost: 6 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (brightness)
- Shelf: 10 kHz, +1.5 dB (shimmer)

### Compression
- Ratio: 3:1
- Threshold: -18 dB
- Attack: 5 ms
- Release: 80 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Threshold: -36 dB
- Attack: 0.3 ms
- Hold: 30 ms
- Release: 100 ms
- Range: 20 dB

### Pan Position
- **Slight left, 10-20 left** (audience perspective). The hi-hat sits to the drummer's left (audience right in drummer perspective), but from the audience view it appears slightly to the left. Keep it subtle to avoid distracting stereo imbalance on constant eighth-note patterns.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-12 to -18 dB** below lead vocal. The hi-hat is largely picked up by overheads. The close mic is used primarily for definition control. Keep it low in the mix; it should add detail, not volume.

---

## Overheads (Drum Kit)

### Frequency Ranges
- **Low bleed**: 80-250 Hz. Contains kick and tom bleed.
- **Kit body**: 250-2 kHz. The overall tonality of the kit.
- **Cymbal definition**: 2-6 kHz. Where cymbal character lives.
- **Air/sparkle**: 8-16 kHz. Brightness and life of cymbals.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 100-150 Hz, 18 dB/oct
- Cut: 250 Hz, -2 dB, Q=1.0 (reduce low bleed)
- Boost: 3 kHz, +1.5 dB, Q=1.0 (cymbal clarity)
- Shelf: 12 kHz, +2 dB (air and sparkle)

### Compression
- Ratio: 3:1
- Threshold: -20 dB
- Attack: 10 ms
- Release: 150 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Not recommended. Overheads should capture the full kit ambience.

### Pan Position
- **Wide stereo pair**:
  - Left overhead: 70-100 left
  - Right overhead: 70-100 right
- This creates the natural stereo image of the full drum kit. Ensure the left/right assignment matches the physical mic placement (spaced pair or XY). Check for phase coherence between the two mics.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-12 to -18 dB** below lead vocal. Overheads provide the overall stereo picture and cymbal presence. They should fill out the kit sound without competing with close mics. In smaller venues, overheads may be the primary drum source and can be pushed closer to -10 dB.

---

## Bass Guitar (DI + Amp)

### Frequency Ranges
- **Sub**: 30-60 Hz. Deep fundamental rumble.
- **Fundamental**: 60-150 Hz. The primary note weight.
- **Mud**: 200-350 Hz. Common problem area.
- **Growl**: 500-900 Hz. Midrange character and string definition.
- **Attack**: 1.5-3 kHz. Finger and pick articulation.
- **Fret noise**: 3-5 kHz. String noise (usually undesirable).

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 30 Hz, 18 dB/oct
- Boost: 80 Hz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (weight)
- Cut: 250 Hz, -3 dB, Q=2.0 (mud cleanup)
- Boost: 700 Hz, +1.5 dB, Q=1.5 (growl)
- Boost: 2.5 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (attack clarity)

### Compression
- Ratio: 4:1
- Threshold: -15 dB
- Attack: 25 ms (preserve pick/finger transient)
- Release: 200 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Threshold: -40 dB
- Attack: 2 ms
- Hold: 80 ms
- Release: 300 ms
- Range: 15 dB (gentle — don't fully close on bass)

### Pan Position
- **Center (0)**. Bass guitar must always be center. Low frequencies are omnidirectional and panning bass creates phase issues in multi-speaker PA systems. The bass and kick together form the mono-compatible rhythmic and harmonic foundation.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-3 to -6 dB** below lead vocal. The bass should lock with the kick drum to form the rhythmic foundation. In funk or R&B, the bass may sit closer to -3 dB; in rock or pop, -4 to -6 dB is typical. The bass-to-kick relationship is critical — they should feel unified.

---

## Electric Guitar

### Frequency Ranges
- **Low-end boom**: 80-200 Hz. Cabinet resonance; usually too much.
- **Body/crunch**: 400-1 kHz. The core tone and character.
- **Presence/bite**: 2-4 kHz. Cut-through in the mix.
- **Harshness**: 5-7 kHz. Can be piercing; often needs taming.
- **Air**: 8-12 kHz. Rarely useful for electric guitar.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 100 Hz, 18 dB/oct
- Cut: 200 Hz, -2 dB, Q=1.5 (reduce cabinet boom)
- Boost: 800 Hz, +1.5 dB, Q=1.5 (body/crunch)
- Boost: 3 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (presence)
- Cut: 6 kHz, -1.5 dB, Q=2.0 (harshness taming)

### Compression
- Ratio: 3:1
- Threshold: -14 dB
- Attack: 15 ms
- Release: 180 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Medium (2)
- Note: Tube amps already compress the signal. Use gentle compression.

### Gate
- Threshold: -45 dB
- Attack: 2 ms
- Hold: 60 ms
- Release: 250 ms
- Range: 10 dB (gentle — prevent noise during pauses, preserve sustain)

### Pan Position
- **Moderate pan, 30-50 left or right**. With two guitars, pan them opposite (e.g., Guitar 1 at 40L, Guitar 2 at 40R) to create width and separation. With a single guitar, pan 20-30 to one side to leave room for other instruments. During solos, consider pulling toward center (10-20 off-center) for focus.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-6 to -10 dB** below lead vocal. Electric guitar should support the vocal, not compete with it. During rhythm sections, keep it further back (-8 to -10 dB). During guitar solos, ride the fader up to -3 to -6 dB below vocal, or even equal to vocal momentarily.

---

## Acoustic Guitar

### Frequency Ranges
- **Boominess**: 80-200 Hz. Body resonance amplified by soundhole.
- **Warmth**: 200-400 Hz. Fullness of the instrument.
- **Nasal**: 500-1 kHz. Mid-range honk.
- **String clarity**: 2-4 kHz. Pick/strum articulation.
- **Sparkle**: 8-16 kHz. String shimmer and brightness.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 80 Hz, 18 dB/oct
- Cut: 100 Hz, -2 dB, Q=1.5 (boominess from body)
- Cut: 250 Hz, -1.5 dB, Q=2.0 (mud reduction)
- Boost: 3 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (string clarity)
- Shelf: 10 kHz, +2 dB (sparkle)

### Compression
- Ratio: 3:1
- Threshold: -18 dB
- Attack: 15 ms
- Release: 150 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Threshold: -42 dB (conservative — avoid cutting off quiet finger-picked passages)
- Attack: 2 ms
- Hold: 80 ms
- Release: 300 ms
- Range: 10 dB

### Pan Position
- **Moderate pan, 30-50 left or right**. If accompanying a vocalist, pan the acoustic guitar opposite to any other melodic instrument for separation. In a singer-songwriter setup with only acoustic guitar and vocal, pan guitar 20-30 to one side with vocal center. In a full band, 40-50 to one side works well.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-8 to -12 dB** below lead vocal. Acoustic guitar is a supporting instrument in a full band context. In an acoustic/singer-songwriter setting, bring it up to -4 to -6 dB below vocal. It should be felt as harmonic support, with strumming patterns audible but not dominant.

---

## Lead Vocal

### Frequency Ranges
- **Proximity effect**: 80-200 Hz. Low-end buildup from close mic technique.
- **Chest resonance**: 200-400 Hz. Warmth and body.
- **Nasal/honk**: 600-1 kHz. The most common vocal problem frequency.
- **Presence**: 2-4 kHz. Intelligibility and cut-through. Human ear is most sensitive here.
- **Sibilance**: 4-8 kHz. "S" and "T" sounds.
- **Air**: 8-16 kHz. Breath, openness, intimacy.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 80 Hz, 18 dB/oct
- Cut: 200 Hz, -2 dB, Q=1.5 (proximity compensation)
- Cut: 800 Hz, -1.5 dB, Q=2.0 (nasal reduction)
- Boost: 3 kHz, +2.5 dB, Q=1.5 (presence for intelligibility)
- Shelf: 10 kHz, +2 dB (air and breath)

### Compression
- Ratio: 3:1
- Threshold: -18 dB (aim for 3-6 dB of gain reduction during loud passages)
- Attack: 10 ms (fast enough to control dynamics, slow enough to preserve articulation)
- Release: 120 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Soft (3)

### Gate
- Threshold: -40 dB
- Attack: 1 ms
- Hold: 80 ms
- Release: 300 ms
- Range: 10 dB (gentle — never fully gate a vocal)

### Pan Position
- **Center (0)**. Always. The lead vocal is the focal point of the mix. Center placement ensures maximum intelligibility and equal coverage across the entire PA system. Never pan the lead vocal off-center.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **0 dB (reference)**. The lead vocal is the reference point for the entire mix. All other instruments are balanced relative to it. Set the lead vocal fader to unity (0 dB) after proper gain staging, and build the rest of the mix around it.

---

## Backing Vocals

### Frequency Ranges
Similar to lead vocal but processed for blend rather than prominence.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 100 Hz, 18 dB/oct (higher than lead — less proximity warmth needed)
- Cut: 200 Hz, -3 dB, Q=1.5 (proximity cleanup)
- Cut: 800 Hz, -2 dB, Q=2.0 (nasal reduction)
- Boost: 3.5 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (blend presence)
- Shelf: 8 kHz, +1.5 dB (air)

### Compression
- Ratio: 3.5:1 (slightly harder than lead for more consistent blend)
- Threshold: -20 dB
- Attack: 8 ms
- Release: 100 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Soft (3)

### Gate
- Threshold: -36 dB
- Attack: 1 ms
- Hold: 60 ms
- Release: 250 ms
- Range: 15 dB

### Pan Position
- **Spread across the stereo field, 50-80 left and right**:
  - With 2 backing vocals: Pan 50L and 50R for a wide, even spread.
  - With 3 backing vocals: Pan 60L, center (0), and 60R.
  - With 4+ backing vocals: Spread evenly across the field from 40L to 40R or wider.
- Backing vocals should create a wide, enveloping harmonic bed behind the centered lead vocal. Avoid panning any backing vocal dead center if it might mask the lead.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-6 to -10 dB** below lead vocal. Backing vocals should support and enrich the lead without competing for attention. During harmony-heavy sections (chorus), they can be pushed to -4 to -6 dB. During verses where the lead is exposed, pull back to -8 to -10 dB or mute.

## Keyboard / Synth

### Frequency Ranges
- **Sub bass** (synth bass): 30-80 Hz
- **Low body**: 80-250 Hz. Pad warmth.
- **Mid presence**: 500-2 kHz. Melodic content.
- **Brightness**: 3-8 kHz. Attack and definition.
- **Air**: 10-16 kHz. Shimmer.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 30-60 Hz (depends on patch — higher for pads, lower for synth bass)
- Cut: 300 Hz, -2 dB, Q=1.5 (mud reduction)
- Boost: 2 kHz, +1.5 dB, Q=1.0 (presence)
- Shelf: 8 kHz, +1 dB (brightness)

### Compression
- Ratio: 2.5:1
- Threshold: -16 dB
- Attack: 15 ms
- Release: 150 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Not typically needed. Synths have clean signal paths.

### Pan Position
- **Moderate pan, 30-50 left or right**. Pan keyboards opposite to guitars for stereo separation. Stereo keyboard patches can be spread with left output panned 40-60L and right output 40-60R. For synth bass patches, keep center (0) like bass guitar. Mono pad sounds work well at 30-40 to one side.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-8 to -12 dB** below lead vocal. Keyboards typically provide harmonic fill and atmosphere. In keyboard-driven arrangements (piano ballad, synth-pop), push to -4 to -6 dB. For pad/texture roles, keep at -10 to -12 dB. Synth bass follows bass guitar level conventions (-3 to -6 dB below vocal).

## Piano (Live/DI)

### Frequency Ranges
- **Low register**: 27-200 Hz. Bass notes fundamental.
- **Body/warmth**: 200-500 Hz. Fullness of the instrument.
- **Clarity**: 1-3 kHz. Note definition and hammer attack.
- **Brilliance**: 4-8 kHz. Upper harmonics and presence.
- **Sparkle**: 8-16 kHz. String shimmer.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 40 Hz, 18 dB/oct
- Cut: 250 Hz, -2 dB, Q=1.5 (reduce mud in live room)
- Boost: 1 kHz, +1 dB, Q=1.5 (body)
- Boost: 5 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.0 (clarity)
- Shelf: 10 kHz, +1.5 dB (sparkle)

### Compression
- Ratio: 2.5:1
- Threshold: -20 dB
- Attack: 20 ms (preserve hammer transient)
- Release: 200 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Not typically needed. Use only if stage bleed is severe.

### Pan Position
- **Slight off-center to moderate pan, 20-40 left or right**. A mono piano DI works well at 20-30 to one side. A stereo-miked grand piano can be spread wider: low strings at 30-40L, high strings at 30-40R. In a piano-vocal duo, keep piano slightly off-center (15-20) to give the vocal clear center space.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-6 to -10 dB** below lead vocal in a full band. In a piano-vocal setting, the piano is the primary accompaniment and should sit at -3 to -5 dB below the vocal. In a full band, pull back to -8 to -10 dB as it becomes harmonic fill.

## Accordion

### Frequency Ranges
- **Low body**: 80-250 Hz. Bellows resonance.
- **Mid body**: 250-1 kHz. Core tone of the reeds.
- **Reed clarity**: 2-5 kHz. Individual reed articulation.
- **Reed harshness**: 5-8 kHz. Can be piercing on certain notes.
- **Air**: 8-12 kHz.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 60 Hz, 18 dB/oct
- Cut: 200 Hz, -2 dB, Q=1.5 (reduce boom)
- Boost: 1 kHz, +1 dB, Q=1.5 (body)
- Boost: 3.5 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (reed clarity)
- Cut: 8 kHz, -1 dB, Q=1.0 (tame harsh reeds)

### Compression
- Ratio: 3:1
- Threshold: -16 dB
- Attack: 10 ms
- Release: 150 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Threshold: -42 dB
- Range: 10 dB

### Pan Position
- **Moderate pan, 20-40 left or right**. Place opposite to guitars or other melodic instruments for separation. In folk or zydeco settings where accordion is the lead instrument, pull closer to center (10-15 off-center).

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-6 to -10 dB** below lead vocal. When the accordion is a featured solo instrument (zydeco, polka, folk), push to -4 to -6 dB. As accompaniment in a full band, keep at -8 to -10 dB.

## Trumpet

### Frequency Ranges
- **Low body**: 150-400 Hz.
- **Warmth/body**: 400-1.5 kHz.
- **Brilliance**: 2-5 kHz. The bright, cutting character.
- **Harshness**: 5-8 kHz. Can be piercing when played loudly.
- **Air**: 8-12 kHz.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 120 Hz, 18 dB/oct
- Cut: 300 Hz, -1.5 dB, Q=1.5 (boxiness)
- Boost: 1.5 kHz, +1.5 dB, Q=1.5 (body warmth)
- Boost: 5 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (brilliance)
- Cut: 8 kHz, -1 dB, Q=2.0 (harshness taming)

### Compression
- Ratio: 3:1
- Threshold: -14 dB
- Attack: 5 ms (fast — trumpet dynamics can be extreme)
- Release: 120 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Threshold: -38 dB
- Attack: 1 ms
- Hold: 60 ms
- Release: 200 ms
- Range: 15 dB

### Pan Position
- **Moderate pan, 30-50 left or right**. In a horn section, spread the horns across the stereo field (e.g., trumpet 40L, trombone 20R, sax 40R). As a solo instrument, pull toward center (10-20 off-center) for focus and impact.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-6 to -10 dB** below lead vocal. Trumpet naturally projects well and cuts through a mix easily due to its strong upper harmonics. Keep it restrained during ensemble passages (-8 to -10 dB). During solos, push to -3 to -6 dB.

## Saxophone

### Frequency Ranges
- **Low body**: 100-300 Hz (varies by type: soprano higher, baritone lower).
- **Honk/nasal**: 250-500 Hz.
- **Body/character**: 500-1.5 kHz. Core tone.
- **Brightness/edge**: 2-5 kHz. Presence and cut.
- **Air/breath**: 6-10 kHz. Breath sound and overtones.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 80 Hz (alto/tenor) / 40 Hz (baritone) / 150 Hz (soprano)
- Cut: 250 Hz, -2 dB, Q=1.5 (honk reduction)
- Boost: 800 Hz, +1.5 dB, Q=1.5 (body)
- Boost: 3 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (presence)
- Shelf: 8 kHz, +1 dB (air)

### Compression
- Ratio: 3:1
- Threshold: -16 dB
- Attack: 8 ms
- Release: 140 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Medium (2)

### Gate
- Threshold: -40 dB
- Attack: 1 ms
- Hold: 70 ms
- Release: 250 ms
- Range: 12 dB

### Pan Position
- **Moderate pan, 30-50 left or right**. In a horn section, spread opposite to other horns. As a solo instrument, pull to 10-20 off-center. Baritone sax, which occupies the low-frequency role in a horn section, can sit closer to center (10-20) like a bass instrument.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-6 to -10 dB** below lead vocal. Saxophone, like trumpet, projects naturally. During ensemble horn parts, keep at -8 to -10 dB. During solos, push to -3 to -6 dB. A baritone sax filling a bass role may need to sit at -4 to -6 dB.

## Violin / Fiddle

### Frequency Ranges
- **Low body**: 200-400 Hz. The warm, woody resonance of the body.
- **Nasal/honk**: 500-1 kHz. Mid-range boxiness, especially on lower strings.
- **Clarity/brilliance**: 2-5 kHz. Bowing articulation and note definition.
- **Harshness/screech**: 5-8 kHz. Can be piercing on high notes or aggressive bowing.
- **Air/shimmer**: 8-14 kHz. String overtones and rosin sound.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 150 Hz, 18 dB/oct (violin fundamental starts around 196 Hz for open G)
- Cut: 400 Hz, -2 dB, Q=1.5 (reduce body boxiness)
- Cut: 800 Hz, -1.5 dB, Q=2.0 (nasal honk reduction)
- Boost: 3 kHz, +2 dB, Q=1.5 (clarity and bow articulation)
- Shelf: 10 kHz, +1.5 dB (air and shimmer)
- Cut: 6 kHz, -1 dB, Q=2.0 (tame screechiness if needed)

### Compression
- Ratio: 3:1
- Threshold: -18 dB
- Attack: 10 ms (preserve bow attack transient)
- Release: 150 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Soft (3)

### Gate
- Threshold: -44 dB (very conservative — violin dynamics range from pianissimo to fortissimo)
- Attack: 1 ms
- Hold: 80 ms
- Release: 300 ms
- Range: 8 dB (very gentle — never cut off quiet passages)

### Pan Position
- **Moderate pan, 20-40 left or right**. As a solo instrument (fiddle in country/folk), pan 15-20 off-center for focus. In an ensemble or string section, follow traditional orchestral seating: first violins 30-50 left. For a single fiddle in a band, pan opposite to other melodic instruments.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-6 to -10 dB** below lead vocal. As a featured solo instrument (fiddle in country, Irish, or folk), push to -3 to -6 dB during solos. As accompaniment, keep at -8 to -10 dB. Violin has a wide dynamic range, so compression is important for maintaining consistent level.

## Strings Section / Ensemble

### Frequency Ranges
- **Low body**: 80-250 Hz. Cello and viola fundamentals, bass warmth.
- **Warmth**: 250-500 Hz. Rich, full ensemble tone.
- **Nasal/boxy**: 500-1 kHz. Can become congested with multiple string voices.
- **Presence**: 2-5 kHz. Bow articulation and note clarity across the section.
- **Air/shimmer**: 8-16 kHz. Rosin, string overtones, and hall ambience.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 60 Hz, 18 dB/oct (preserve cello low fundamentals down to ~65 Hz)
- Cut: 300 Hz, -2 dB, Q=1.0 (reduce muddiness from multiple instruments)
- Cut: 700 Hz, -1.5 dB, Q=1.5 (nasal reduction in ensemble)
- Boost: 3.5 kHz, +1.5 dB, Q=1.0 (ensemble clarity and articulation)
- Shelf: 10 kHz, +2 dB (air and string shimmer)

### Compression
- Ratio: 2.5:1
- Threshold: -20 dB
- Attack: 15 ms (preserve ensemble attack and dynamic expression)
- Release: 200 ms
- Detector: RMS
- Knee: Soft (3)
- Note: Compress gently. Strings rely on dynamics for musical expression. Over-compression destroys the natural swell and phrasing.

### Gate
- Not recommended. String ensembles have continuous, flowing passages with very soft dynamics that gates would destroy.

### Pan Position
- **Wide stereo spread following orchestral convention (audience perspective)**:
  - First violins: 40-60 left
  - Second violins: 20-40 left
  - Violas: 10-30 right
  - Cellos: 30-50 right
  - Double bass: 40-60 right (or center if providing bass function)
- For a stereo pair of room mics on a string section: pan 60-80 left and right to capture the natural ensemble image.

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-8 to -14 dB** below lead vocal. String sections typically provide lush harmonic padding and emotional texture. They should be felt as much as heard. During string-featured passages (intros, interludes, dramatic swells), push to -4 to -8 dB. During vocal sections, pull back to -10 to -14 dB to stay behind the vocal.

## Playback / Backing Track

### Frequency Ranges
Pre-mixed stereo content. Full frequency range 20 Hz - 20 kHz.

### EQ Moves
- HPF: 20 Hz (only remove subsonic content)
- Minimal EQ recommended — the track is already mixed
- Optional: gentle cut at 200 Hz, -1 dB, Q=0.7 (reduce low-mid buildup with live bass)

### Compression
- Not recommended. Playback tracks are already mastered with compression applied.
- Exception: If the track has extreme dynamic range, use ratio 2:1, threshold -10 dB.

### Gate
- Not needed. Signal is clean and continuous.

### Pan Position
- **Stereo, as provided**. Left output panned hard left (100L), right output panned hard right (100R). The playback track is already mixed and mastered with its own stereo image. Do not alter the panning. If the track must be summed to mono for system compatibility, ensure the content is mono-compatible (check for phase cancellation).

### Fader Level (Relative to Lead Vocal)
- **-6 to -12 dB** below lead vocal, depending on content. Playback tracks vary widely: a click-and-cue track for the band only should be routed to monitors, not FOH. Musical backing tracks (strings, pads, extra production) should sit at -8 to -12 dB as they support the live performance. Tracks that replace a missing band member (e.g., backing track bass) should follow that instrument's normal level convention.
